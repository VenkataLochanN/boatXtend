Market Context and the Illusion of Brand-Specific Engineering

To successfully engineer custom firmware for a commercial smartwatch, one must first deconstruct the supply chain and understand the origin of the hardware. The economic model of the budget wearable market relies almost entirely on Original Design Manufacturers (ODMs) and white-label engineering. Brands such as boAt, BeatXP, Ryze, Cove, and numerous others do not typically design their silicon, motherboards, or software stacks in-house. Instead, they license complete hardware and software platforms from centralized manufacturers, applying cosmetic alterations to the chassis and basic branding modifications to the companion applications.   

The boAt Xtend, heavily marketed with features including built-in Amazon Alexa integration, 5ATM water resistance, a 1.69-inch capacitive LCD display, and multi-metric physiological monitoring (SpO2, heart rate, and stress tracking), serves as an archetype of this phenomenon. This attribution is the foundational step in custom software development. The firmware ecosystem is tethered to the ODM (IDO) rather than the consumer brand (boAt). Consequently, the official companion applications—branded as "boAt Wave" or "boAt Crest"—are merely reskinned iterations of "VeryFit" or "VeryFitPro," the proprietary applications developed by IDO to interface with their generic hardware platforms. By understanding that the boAt Xtend operates on the IDO VeryFit protocol architecture, developers can leverage existing reverse-engineering efforts directed at the broader IDO ecosystem to facilitate custom firmware deployment.   

Hardware Architecture and Microcontroller Identification

The development of embedded software requires absolute parity with the underlying hardware architecture. A custom firmware binary compiled for the wrong processor architecture or linked to incorrect memory offsets will invariably result in a hard fault, effectively bricking the device. Hardware teardowns of the boAt Xtend and analogous devices (such as the BeatXP or boAt Ultima Chronos) reveal highly optimized, space-constrained internal architectures.   

The primary components of interest for firmware engineers include the System-on-Chip (SoC), the display controller matrix, the power management integrated circuit (PMIC), and the physiological sensor array (photoplethysmography or PPG sensors, and multiaxial accelerometers). While IDO utilizes various silicon providers depending on global supply chain constraints, extensive reverse engineering and documentation analysis indicate the prevalent use of two primary microcontroller families in their advanced wearable tiers: the Nordic Semiconductor nRF52 series (e.g., nRF52840 or the older nRF51822 found in the ID107HR) and the Ambiq Micro Apollo 3 Blue.   

The Ambiq Apollo 3 Blue Microcontroller Architecture

The Ambiq Apollo 3 Blue SoC represents the vanguard of ultra-low-power edge computing and is a highly probable candidate for driving devices like the boAt Xtend. The chip features an ARM Cortex-M4F core equipped with a dedicated Floating-Point Unit (FPU), operating at up to 96 MHz. The inclusion of the FPU is strictly necessary for real-time mathematical operations required by the smartwatch, such as calculating heart rate variability (HRV) from raw PPG optical data streams or processing audio samples for the built-in Alexa voice assistant via a Pulse Density Modulation (PDM) microphone.   

The defining characteristic of the Apollo 3 platform is Ambiq's proprietary Sub-Threshold Power Optimized Technology (SPOT). This microarchitectural design allows the logic gates within the silicon to operate at voltage levels significantly below the standard threshold, resulting in order-of-magnitude reductions in energy consumption during both active execution and deep sleep states. To achieve the advertised 7-to-10-day battery life on a highly constrained 300mAh lithium-polymer cell, the custom firmware must meticulously manage these power states.

The Nordic Semiconductor nRF52 Alternative

In iterations where supply chain logistics favor Nordic Semiconductor, the IDO architecture may utilize the nRF52 series (such as the nRF52832 or nRF52840). The nRF52 series similarly relies on an ARM Cortex-M4F core but utilizes a fundamentally different software stack, predominantly relying on Nordic's "SoftDevice" architecture—a pre-compiled, proprietary binary that handles the lower layers of the Bluetooth Low Energy protocol stack. When developing custom firmware for an nRF52-based device, the application code must be linked to sit above the SoftDevice in the memory map, and OTA updates are handled via the Nordic Device Firmware Update (DFU) protocol rather than Ambiq's AMOTA.   

Reverse Engineering the Bluetooth Low Energy (BLE) Protocol

The core objective of flashing custom software over Bluetooth without physically disassembling the device relies entirely on exploiting the OEM's Over-The-Air (OTA) update mechanisms. This requires a profound understanding of how the official companion applications (boAt Wave / VeryFit) communicate with the smartwatch.

The boAt Xtend, operating within the IDO ecosystem, utilizes a proprietary, highly structured BLE communication protocol identified in reverse engineering literature as the "V3 protocol". This protocol operates over standard BLE Generic Attribute Profile (GATT) characteristics, utilizing specific Services and Characteristics to multiplex commands, physiological data, and firmware payloads over the wireless link.   

Architectural Breakdown of the V3 Protocol

The V3 protocol is engineered to handle the inherent limitations of Bluetooth Low Energy, specifically the Maximum Transmission Unit (MTU) size constraints, which typically limit payload sizes to 20 to 244 bytes per packet. Because complex commands, historical health data syncs, and firmware binaries vastly exceed this limit, the V3 protocol implements a fragmentation and reassembly mechanism at the application layer.   

Packet capture analysis, utilizing tools such as Wireshark paired with an HCI (Host Controller Interface) snoop log from an Android device or a dedicated BLE sniffer like Bettercap, reveals the distinct signature of the V3 protocol. Every command sequence initiated over this protocol is prefixed with a specific hexadecimal byte: 0x33 (which translates to the ASCII character '3').   

To ensure that the receiving microcontroller can successfully delineate the beginning of a new, discrete command from the continuation payload of a previously fragmented packet, the protocol relies on a hardcoded, 32-bit synchronization preamble. This preamble is defined as the hexadecimal sequence 0xDAADDAAD.

Systemic Vulnerabilities and OTA Exploitation

The most critical finding derived from the reverse engineering of the IDO V3 protocol is the complete absence of robust cryptographic security. While the protocol utilizes a CRC16 checksum, this mechanism is designed purely for environmental error detection (e.g., radio interference) and provides zero cryptographic assurance.   

Modern, secure embedded systems employ asymmetric cryptography—such as Elliptic Curve Digital Signature Algorithm (ECDSA) or RSA-2048—within their bootloaders. In a secure paradigm, the OEM signs the compiled firmware binary with a private key, and the device's bootloader utilizes a hardcoded public key to verify the signature before allowing the flash memory to be overwritten.

The IDO ID206 / boAt Xtend architecture omits this critical security layer. The bootloader will accept, write, and execute any properly formatted binary payload delivered via the V3 OTA protocol, provided the CRC16 matches the payload. As noted by researchers analyzing the VeryFit ecosystem, "Whatever you want you can get it - there's no protection".   

This architectural oversight is the precise mechanism that enables the deployment of custom firmware via Bluetooth. An independent developer can compile a completely proprietary operating system, wrap it in the requisite V3 protocol headers (initiating the OTA sequence with 0x33, DAADDAAD, and the correct Command ID), and the smartwatch's bootloader will unconditionally install it.   

Firmware Extraction and Diagnostic Dump Procedures

Before writing custom software, it is imperative to extract and analyze the existing OEM firmware. A binary dump of the factory software provides the memory map, the driver initialization sequences for undocumented proprietary hardware (such as the specific ST7789 LCD variant or the Si1142/similar PPG sensor), and a pristine copy of the original bootloader.   

Software-Based Extraction via BLE

For IDO-based devices, the lack of read-protection on certain memory sectors allows for software-based firmware extraction over the BLE connection. Open-source repositories, such as xssfox/idowatch, provide Python utilities explicitly designed for this purpose. The primary script, dump.py, leverages cross-platform Python BLE libraries (such as Bleak) to establish a GATT connection with the boAt Xtend.   

The extraction script functions by exploiting the V3 protocol's memory read commands. It authenticates with the smartwatch, initiates a loop that iterates through the internal flash memory address space of the MCU, and sends sequential read requests. The watch dutifully responds with fragments of its own firmware, which the Python script reassembles on the host machine into a complete .bin file. This process can take several hours due to BLE bandwidth limitations but completely bypasses the need for physical disassembly.   

Hardware-Based Extraction via Serial Wire Debug (SWD)

If the device is in a "bricked" state, or if a specific OEM firmware update patches the BLE read vulnerability, developers must resort to hardware-based extraction. This necessitates the physical disassembly of the boAt Xtend chassis to access the motherboard.   

Hardware teardown schematics indicate that the ID206 motherboards feature microscopic test points corresponding to the Serial Wire Debug (SWD) interface—specifically SWDIO (Serial Wire Debug Data Input/Output), SWCLK (Serial Wire Clock), GND, and VDD. By meticulously soldering ultra-fine gauge wire to these test points, developers can interface directly with the Ambiq Apollo 3 or Nordic MCU using a hardware debug probe.   

Industry-standard probes such as the Segger J-Link (often used with the Ambiq Apollo 3 EVB) or the open-source Black Magic Probe are utilized for this process. The Black Magic Probe, specifically from release version 2.0.0 onwards, natively supports the Ambiq Apollo 3 architecture. By connecting the probe and launching the GNU Debugger (GDB), developers can issue direct memory access commands (e.g., dump memory firmware.bin 0x00000000 0x00080000) to extract the entire flash contents.   

Once extracted, the binary dump is loaded into reverse-engineering frameworks like Ghidra or IDA Pro. By applying the ARM Cortex-M architecture definitions, researchers can decompile the machine code to identify the exact memory-mapped registers used to toggle the display, process I2C sensor interrupts, and manage the Bluetooth radio stack.

Establishing the Custom Firmware Development Environment

Creating a custom operating system for an embedded device is a highly rigorous process that requires a specialized toolchain configured explicitly for the target MCU architecture. The development environment varies significantly depending on whether the silicon is an Ambiq Apollo 3 or a Nordic nRF52.

Toolchain Configuration for the Ambiq Apollo 3 Architecture

If the hardware analysis confirms the presence of an Ambiq Apollo 3 Blue SoC, the primary development framework is the AmbiqSuite Software Development Kit (SDK). The AmbiqSuite SDK provides the essential Hardware Abstraction Layer (HAL) necessary to interact safely with the MCU's complex power management systems. Attempting to write bare-metal register manipulations without the HAL often leads to unpredictable behavior, particularly concerning the Simo Buck converter, which must be perfectly tuned to prevent rapid battery drainage or thermal runaway.   

Setting up the bare-metal C/C++ development environment requires the following prerequisites :   

AmbiqSuite SDK: The core library containing the Apollo 3 HAL, peripheral drivers (SPI, I2C, UART), and the integrated BLE stack implementations (e.g., the Cordio stack).   

ARM GCC Toolchain: Specifically, the arm-none-eabi-gcc compiler (version 8-2018-q4-major or compatible is generally recommended for API stability). This cross-compiler translates the high-level C code into the specific ARM Cortex-M4F machine code required by the processor.   

Make Build System: The AmbiqSuite relies heavily on GNU Make to track compilation targets, manage dependencies, and link the object files according to the provided linker scripts.   

Python 3: Required for post-compilation processing, specifically generating the OTA bootloader packages.   

Alternatively, developers operating within a Windows environment frequently utilize the Keil uVision MDK (Microcontroller Development Kit). This requires the installation of the specific Device Family Pack (DFP) for the Ambiq Micro MCU series to provide the IDE with the correct register definitions and flash algorithms.   

When authoring the custom firmware, developers must exercise extreme caution regarding the memory map. The custom application code must not overwrite the Secure Bootloader (SBL) or the critical INFO0 configuration block. The linker script (.ld file) must be meticulously configured to place the application binary at the specific memory offset expected by the SBL, ensuring a successful boot sequence.   

Modern Embedded Ecosystems: Rust and Zephyr RTOS

Writing bare-metal C code for a smartwatch is highly complex, particularly when attempting to manage asynchronous Bluetooth events, display framebuffers, and sensor data polling concurrently. Therefore, modern embedded developers frequently leverage Real-Time Operating Systems (RTOS) or safe-systems programming languages.

The Rust programming language has experienced massive adoption in the embedded hardware space due to its strict memory safety guarantees and zero-cost abstractions. The rust-embedded community has developed specific Peripheral Access Crates (PAC) for this hardware, notably ambiq-apollo3-pac and ambiq-apollo3p-pac. These crates, automatically generated from the manufacturer's System View Description (SVD) files using the svd2rust utility, provide inherently safe interfaces to the hardware registers of the Apollo 3 MCU. Utilizing Embedded Rust allows for the rapid development of robust firmware with a mathematically proven absence of buffer overflows or dangling pointers—common vectors for catastrophic crashes in C-based embedded systems.   

For the operating system layer, the Zephyr RTOS is highly recommended. Zephyr provides extensive, natively integrated support for Bluetooth Low Energy and includes generic, highly optimized drivers for SPI TFT displays and I2C physiological sensors. Porting Zephyr to the boAt Xtend requires the creation of a custom device tree (.dts) file. This file acts as a hardware manifest, mapping the MCU's logical GPIO pins to the specific physical peripherals identified during the hardware teardown and firmware dump analysis, allowing the RTOS to seamlessly interact with the watch's components.

Payload Generation and Over-The-Air (OTA) Flashing Mechanics

Once the custom firmware source code has been successfully compiled and linked into a raw executable binary format (.bin or .hex), it cannot simply be sent directly to the watch. It must be cryptographically packaged (or properly formatted) into a payload that the specific bootloader architecture recognizes and accepts. This packaging process is entirely dependent on the underlying hardware platform.

The Ambiq Micro Over-The-Air (AMOTA) Process

For devices utilizing the Apollo 3 architecture, the OTA update must conform to the Ambiq AMOTA protocol. The raw compiled binary must be wrapped with specific metadata headers that the Ambiq Secure Bootloader expects to parse before initiating the flash write sequence.

The AmbiqSuite SDK provides an automated Python-based scripting toolchain located within the tools/apollo3_amota/scripts directory to handle this formatting. By executing the make command with the appropriate board definition variable (e.g., make Apollo4_BOARD=apollo4b_blue_evb), the toolchain processes the raw .bin file.   

This process outputs an update_binary_apollo4_blue.bin (or the Apollo 3 equivalent). This generated file contains the necessary memory alignment padding, the reset vector addresses, and the CRC metadata required for a successful flash cycle by the SBL.   

The Nordic Device Firmware Update (DFU) Process

If the hardware revision of the boAt Xtend utilizes a Nordic nRF52 MCU, the firmware must be packaged using the heavily standardized Nordic DFU protocol. The Nordic SDK provides a dedicated command-line utility called nrfutil for this exact purpose.   

The developer executes the following command structure to generate the OTA zip package:nrfutil nrf5sdk-tools pkg generate --hw-version 51 --sd-req 0x80 --bootloader-version <version> --application <custom_firmware.hex> custom_update.zip.   

This command generates a highly structured ZIP archive. Inside this archive resides the new firmware image (converted to .bin), a critical initialization packet (.dat file) containing the CRC and size metrics, and a manifest file detailing the package format. The --sd-req flag is particularly crucial; it specifies the required SoftDevice (the Nordic BLE protocol stack) version that must already be present on the device for the firmware to execute correctly.   

Transmitting the Payload via Bluetooth (The Flashing Execution)

With the appropriately formatted update package generated (either AMOTA or Nordic DFU), the final execution phase is transmitting it to the boAt Xtend over the air. Because the custom firmware is unsigned and unrecognized by the OEM, the official "boAt Wave" or "boAt Crest" applications will actively reject the file. Therefore, the developer must bypass the official application entirely and communicate with the watch programmatically.   

A custom Python script, utilizing the Bleak asynchronous BLE library, must be authored to orchestrate the transmission. The script executes the following state machine:

Scan and Authenticate: The script scans the local BLE spectrum, identifies the boAt Xtend by its MAC address, and establishes a connection.

Trigger DFU Mode: The script constructs a specific V3 protocol command packet (prefixed with 0x33 and the DAADDAAD preamble) directed at the device's system configuration GATT characteristic. This command intentionally triggers a software reset, forcing the MCU to reboot out of the main application space and into the bootloader (DFU mode).   

Payload Transmission: Once the device reboots into the bootloader, it advertises a different set of BLE services. The script reconnects to these new services and begins sequentially writing chunks of the update_binary.bin or Nordic .zip archive to the designated OTA GATT characteristic. The script must carefully manage the BLE MTU size, ensuring no packet exceeds the allowable byte limit, and await acknowledgment from the watch before sending the next chunk.

Verification and Reboot: After the entire payload is transmitted, the script issues a final execution command. The bootloader calculates the CRC of the received payload, verifies it against the transmitted metadata, overwrites the primary application space in the flash memory, and reboots the MCU into the newly installed custom operating system.

For users who prefer to avoid writing custom Python transmission scripts, existing tools like dfu-util-static.exe (for generic DFU implementations) or mobile applications like Nordic's nRF Connect can be utilized to push the binaries manually, provided the developer knows the exact UUIDs of the required GATT characteristics.   

Ecosystem Integration: The Role of Gadgetbridge

Replacing the "shit" OEM firmware successfully resolves the issues of unoptimized watch software and restrictive user interfaces. However, a smartwatch possesses limited utility in isolation; it must communicate with a smartphone to receive push notifications, synchronize the real-time clock (RTC), control media playback, and transmit aggregated physiological data. The proprietary "boAt Wave" and "VeryFit" applications are inherently closed-source, enforcing mandatory cloud account creation, continuous telemetry collection, and frequently embedding advertisements within the user interface.   

To create a fully sovereign, privacy-respecting wearable ecosystem, the newly flashed custom firmware should be deliberately engineered to interface with Gadgetbridge. Gadgetbridge is a highly regarded, open-source Android application that acts as a universal companion app for smart bands and watches, operating completely independent of vendor lock-in, proprietary cloud servers, or data telemetry.   

Currently, Gadgetbridge natively supports over 143 gadgets perfectly and possesses partial implementation support for 122 others. It natively supports various generic BLE communication protocols, including advanced Garmin encapsulation methods and older IDO implementations. Crucially, the Gadgetbridge implementation for IDO-based hardware already understands the underlying structure of the V3 protocol.   

By ensuring that the custom firmware's Bluetooth stack adheres to a known, open communication standard (such as the open Gadgetbridge protocol, or by explicitly emulating the GATT profiles of a well-supported device like the Pebble or Amazfit Bip), developers can achieve seamless smartphone integration. This allows the watch to receive incoming call alerts, SMS parsing, and weather updates without requiring the developer to author a companion Android or iOS application from scratch, thereby completing the hardware liberation cycle.   

Second and Third-Order Implications

The ability to completely replace the firmware on a mass-market, highly subsidized IoT device carries profound implications for the broader technology ecosystem, extending far beyond the scope of hobbyist modifications or consumer dissatisfaction.

Security and Network Vulnerability Analysis

The primary technical enabler of this custom firmware deployment is a critical security vulnerability: the absolute failure of the OEM to implement cryptographic signature verification within the bootloader architecture. While this failure is highly advantageous for right-to-repair advocates and open-source developers seeking to maximize the utility of their purchased hardware, it represents a catastrophic structural flaw from an enterprise and consumer security perspective.   

If an arbitrary user or developer can flash unauthenticated firmware via a standard Bluetooth connection, a malicious actor possesses the exact same capability. A compromised smartwatch, outfitted with malicious custom firmware, can be weaponized to act as a covert ambient listening device (utilizing the built-in Alexa PDM microphone), silently exfiltrate sensitive physiological data or GPS location history, or serve as a persistent, trusted pivot point to bypass perimeter security within a corporate network.   

Furthermore, insecure IoT devices are frequently conscripted into autonomous botnets designed to execute Distributed Denial of Service (DDoS) attacks. As cyber security literature notes, the inadequate implementation of Manufacturer Usage Description (MUD) attributes and a systemic lack of Trust Attribute labeling leave IoT ecosystems highly vulnerable to MITM (Man-In-The-Middle) attacks, SQL injections, and backdoor command injections. The boAt Xtend, running on the vulnerable IDO ID206 platform, exemplifies the consumer electronics industry's pervasive tendency to prioritize rapid time-to-market and low unit production costs over fundamental, hardware-level cryptographic security.   

The Right to Repair and Device Longevity

Conversely, the lack of security lockdowns inadvertently champions the "Right to Repair" movement and the mitigation of global electronic waste. Budget consumer electronics typically suffer from extremely abbreviated support lifecycles; ODMs frequently abandon firmware updates and shut down companion app API servers within two to three years of a product's initial launch. When the proprietary servers for applications like "boAt Wave" inevitably go offline, the hardware is traditionally rendered obsolete and functionally useless, despite the physical components (the highly efficient Cortex-M4F processor, the vibrant capacitive LCD, and the lithium-polymer battery) remaining entirely viable.   

The democratization of custom firmware development, combined with the utilization of open-source companion applications like Gadgetbridge, effectively decouples the hardware's operational lifespan from the OEM's planned economic obsolescence cycle. A boAt Xtend running a custom, community-maintained Real-Time Operating System can continue to function indefinitely, providing users with absolute data sovereignty and extending the ecological utility of the physical hardware for a decade or more.   

Shifts in Open-Source Hardware Development and TinyML

The methodologies outlined in this technical analysis reflect a massive paradigm shift in the embedded systems and academic research communities. Historically, developing advanced wearable prototypes or IoT edge-computing devices required designing custom printed circuit boards (PCBs) from scratch—a process requiring significant capital expenditure for fabrication, component sourcing, and electrical compliance testing.

Currently, developers are increasingly adopting a "software-defined hardware" approach. By purchasing mass-produced, highly subsidized consumer electronics (such as a $35 boAt smartwatch)  and reverse-engineering the firmware protocols, researchers obtain a highly miniaturized, battery-optimized development board for a fraction of the cost of a traditional Ambiq EVB or Sparkfun Artemis board.   

This phenomenon democratizes access to advanced IoT development platforms and significantly accelerates academic research in fields such as tinyML (Machine Learning on edge microcontrollers). The Apollo 3 MCU, with its Cortex-M4F core, is heavily utilized in TinyML applications, allowing developers to run complex TensorFlow Lite models directly on the wrist for advanced gesture recognition or localized voice processing without relying on cloud computation. By flashing custom firmware to a boAt Xtend, an off-the-shelf consumer toy is transformed into a highly sophisticated, open-source computational research tool.
